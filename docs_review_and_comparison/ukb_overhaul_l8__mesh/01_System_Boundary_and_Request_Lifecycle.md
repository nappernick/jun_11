# Section 01 — System Boundary and Request Lifecycle

This is the opening section of the Skywalker architecture series, and it does one job: it fixes the runtime boundary before any later section reasons about storage, retrieval mechanics, or client integration. The boundary has to be set first because a soft boundary is expensive later. Leave it implicit and subsequent sections drift into the wrong system, assign ownership to the wrong component, or quietly make the retrieval backend responsible for conversational behavior that belongs to QuickSuite or to the Slack-side agent. Everything downstream inherits the boundary drawn here.

## Tenets

The series resolves its trade-offs against five tenets. Tenets are not values; they are decision rules that tell you what gets sacrificed when two good things conflict. Every load-bearing decision in later sections traces back to one of these.

**We prefer controlled accuracy over broad coverage when they conflict.** The highest-value questions are the ones where the program is judged most tightly, and those are exactly the questions where surrendering control to a black box is least acceptable. Skywalker owns the path that matters most and borrows the path that matters less.

**We prefer backend honesty over always-answer confidence when evidence is weak.** Abstention is a feature, not a failure. A retrieval layer that returns "no grounded answer" when the evidence does not clear the bar is more valuable than one that manufactures confidence, because a confidently wrong answer to an employee about their pay, their visa, or their expense policy is the worst outcome the system can produce.

**We prefer the simplicity of a small owned corpus over sophisticated incremental machinery.** The owned corpus is on the order of ten documents. That fact licenses a daily full rebuild instead of incremental mutation tracking, and the architecture takes the simpler path deliberately rather than building machinery the corpus size does not justify.

**We prefer identity-scoped correctness over generic helpfulness.** The right answer depends on who is asking — their job level, their geography, their employee class. An answer that ignores those dimensions is not a neutral, general-purpose answer. It is wrong-shaped for the person who asked. The architecture treats scope as a correctness input, not as an optional refinement.

**We prefer deterministic retrieval over conversational behavior.** Skywalker selects and ranks evidence; the agent layers above it author prose, manage dialogue, and hold conversation state. Keeping that line sharp is what lets the backend stay testable and the agents stay flexible.

## 1. System boundary: what Skywalker is and is not

Skywalker is the retrieval backend behind an MCP surface for an internal Amazon assistant that serves travel, events, and expense documentation. MCP — the Model Context Protocol — is the contract through which an agent calls a tool server; for our purposes it means Skywalker exposes a small set of search tools that any approved agent can invoke. Skywalker is not the conversational brain. It does not own prompt strategy, dialogue flow, conversation memory, or user-facing prose. It owns identity-aware scoping, routing across two retrieval arms, normalization of heterogeneous candidates into one comparable form, reranking, retrieval-layer abstain behavior, and the structured response that downstream agents consume.

That split is operational, not cosmetic. The backend is deliberately more deterministic than the agent layer above it. The quality target is not "sound helpful in chat." The target is narrower and harder: return the right, properly scoped evidence for the employee asking, or abstain when the system cannot do that safely.

Stated positively, Skywalker owns a specific chain of runtime responsibilities. It exposes an MCP search surface. It accepts a user question plus one of the supported identity entry modes. It resolves identity-derived scope through PAPI — the internal People API — when the caller did not already supply that scope directly. It runs a hybrid routing gate to decide whether a request is Top-50-shaped: cosine similarity against a static in-memory variant set first, with a Cohere Rerank v4 cross-encoder second stage on a dedicated SageMaker endpoint, invoked only on cosine-ambiguous cases. It decides whether the request runs through the FAQ arm alone or through both arms concurrently. The FAQ arm itself runs hybrid retrieval — BM25 lexical matching fused with FAISS cosine vector similarity — against the AOSS evidence index, the two legs combined by an OpenSearch search pipeline. It gathers candidates from the participating arm or arms, normalizes them into one common shape, reranks them through Cohere Rerank 4 Pro on SageMaker, and returns a structured result bundle. It decides when the retrieval layer should abstain rather than pretend it has sufficient evidence.

Stated negatively, the non-goals matter just as much, because they keep later sections from smearing responsibility onto the wrong component. Skywalker does not own the user-facing conversation. It does not manage multi-turn state. It does not decide how a final answer is worded for Slack or QuickSuite, and it does not own prompt orchestration for those surfaces. It does not own UKB internals, because UKB is an external MCP server Skywalker calls. It does not own PAPI. It does not own the post-launch human review process that validates answer quality in production, and it does not own the evaluation harness — both are real program activities, but neither is part of Skywalker's runtime ownership.

The result is a deliberate division of labor. Skywalker is the scoped retrieval and evidence-selection layer. QuickSuite and the Slack-side agent are the conversational layers. PAPI is the identity-enrichment dependency. UKB is the broad black-box retrieval dependency for the general arm. Later sections detail the owned FAQ ingest and storage path, but the boundary fixes one thing now: the owned FAQ path is part of Skywalker, while agent behavior is not.

One transport fact belongs in the boundary statement rather than only in the client sections, because it shapes the security posture of the whole system. All three production client paths reach Skywalker's MCP server through **Amazon MCP Gateway**, the internal paved path for hosting and consuming MCP servers across Amazon. The Slack inline-agent orchestrator and the UAT inline-agent orchestrator, both hosted on an IAM fleet, use the gateway's CloudAuth-inbound route at `https://api.mcp.asbx.aws.dev/ca/mcp/{registry}/{server}`, each registered as a CloudAuth-modeled AAA application. QuickSuite uses the gateway's Federate-OAuth-inbound route at `https://api.mcp.asbx.aws.dev/federate/mcp/{registry}/{server}`. The gateway endpoint is a public DNS name with authentication enforced at the gateway, so Skywalker inherits a public-with-auth posture from the gateway's natural shape; the closed set of supported auth shapes — CloudAuth, Federate OAuth, and SigV4 for any future caller it suits — keeps the authorization boundary against any caller outside Amazon's auth platforms (see [research_grounding_docs/API_13_Network_Fabric_and_Internet_Exposure_Posture.md](research_grounding_docs/API_13_Network_Fabric_and_Internet_Exposure_Posture.md)). There is no wrapper Lambda between any client and the MCP server, and no task-oriented translation tool: every client reads the same `tools/list` and chooses among the three core tools directly. On the Slack and UAT paths, CloudAuth OBO carries the calling orchestrator's AAA application identity to Skywalker, and TransitiveAuth carries the originating human end-user's identity as a separate token alongside it, so Skywalker reads the human alias server-side from the TransitiveAuth claims rather than from MCP tool arguments. On the QuickSuite path, MCP Gateway does not currently propagate originating end-user identity downstream on the Federate-inbound combination, so QuickSuite carries identity in MCP tool arguments through its own integration-specific channel. Consolidating onto one transport with one Bindle authorization surface is the simplification that removed the earlier design's wrapper-and-AgentCore-Gateway complexity. See [research_grounding_docs/API_01_MCP_Server_Protocol.md](research_grounding_docs/API_01_MCP_Server_Protocol.md) for the core MCP contract, [research_grounding_docs/API_08_QuickSuite_MCP_Gateway_Integration.md](research_grounding_docs/API_08_QuickSuite_MCP_Gateway_Integration.md) for the QuickSuite integration, and [research_grounding_docs/API_14_CloudAuth_OBO_and_TransitiveAuth_Integration.md](research_grounding_docs/API_14_CloudAuth_OBO_and_TransitiveAuth_Integration.md) for the identity propagation on the Slack and UAT paths.

## 2. Inputs, outputs, and contracts

The MCP boundary exists so multiple callers can share one retrieval backend without each re-implementing policy-aware retrieval. The contract at that boundary therefore has to reflect the real identity inputs the system receives, not an idealized one-size request.

Skywalker supports three conceptual entry modes, and the difference between them is entirely about where scope comes from. The first takes a query plus an alias and resolves the employee's scoped metadata through PAPI. This is the Slack-side entry mode. The second takes a query plus an employee identifier and resolves the same metadata through PAPI. The third takes a query plus the already-resolved scoping fields and bypasses PAPI entirely; it exists because some callers already know the scope they want, and the architecture should not force a needless PAPI round-trip when the caller deliberately supplies the decisive fields. QuickSuite enters through the alias mode, because its Federate JWT carries the user's Midway login as the `sub` claim and nothing more — the same alias-only identity shape Slack provides.

The active scope is the triple established in the project definition: **geography, level, and employee class** — where level means the employee's job level and employee class distinguishes manager from individual contributor and from the other real employment categories the data carries. These three are the active correctness dimensions for retrieval, and they are grounded in the ingestion code rather than asserted abstractly: the corpus stores them on every fragment, sourced from the COREx `geography` field, the `system_job-level` metadata field, and the `system_employee-class` metadata field respectively (see [03_Top_50_FAQ_Controlled_Corpus_Ingestion_and_Storage.md](03_Top_50_FAQ_Controlled_Corpus_Ingestion_and_Storage.md)). A response that ignores them is not "generic." It is wrong-shaped for the employee asking, and the system treats it that way.

The input contract is therefore simple in concept even though the concrete wire schema is finalized in [02_MCP_Entry_Contract_and_Identity_Scoping.md](02_MCP_Entry_Contract_and_Identity_Scoping.md). A valid request entering the orchestration layer carries a query and either an identity token resolvable into scope or the resolved scope itself. It may also carry caller-specific request metadata, but those fields do not change retrieval semantics.

The output contract matters as much as the input contract, because it is what keeps Skywalker from drifting into agent behavior. Skywalker does not return an authored chat answer. It returns a structured evidence bundle. At minimum that bundle contains the selected result set in ranked order, routing metadata explaining which path produced the candidates, identifiers or links that let the caller refer back to source material, and a retrieval-layer signal indicating whether the system found answerable evidence or is abstaining. The final response schema is pinned in [02_MCP_Entry_Contract_and_Identity_Scoping.md](02_MCP_Entry_Contract_and_Identity_Scoping.md); the boundary decision fixed here is that Skywalker returns retrieval output for agents to consume, never finished conversational prose for end users to read.

One internal contract is also fixed at the concept level here: the reranker scores one kind of object. Both the owned FAQ arm and the UKB arm may participate in a single request, but neither may hand opaque, arm-specific result objects straight into the reranker. Before reranking, candidates are normalized into a common representation so the reranker scores the same shape regardless of source. The exact candidate schema is detailed in [07_Reranking_Candidate_Unification_and_Abstain_Behavior.md](07_Reranking_Candidate_Unification_and_Abstain_Behavior.md), but the existence of a common schema is not optional. Without it, the reranker is a fiction rather than a true common scoring surface.

## 3. Fixed decisions

The following decisions are settled firmly enough that later sections treat them as constraints rather than open debates. Each is stated as a decision: what was decided, why this and not the alternative, what depends on it, and — most important for an architecture that will live under implementation pressure — what specific evidence would reopen it. The full decision log with reopen triggers lives in [10_Calibration_Relitigation_Triggers_and_Decision_Log.md](10_Calibration_Relitigation_Triggers_and_Decision_Log.md); the summaries here are the load-bearing version.

**Decision 1: Skywalker is a two-arm retrieval system.** One arm is the controlled Top-50 FAQ path; the other is the general path through UKB. The FAQ arm exists because the highest-value, highest-accuracy questions are precisely where the program cannot afford to surrender control to a black box. The UKB arm exists because UKB already exposes broad documentation retrieval over the travel, events, and expense domains through an MCP surface, so Skywalker does not rebuild a general retrieval stack it would otherwise have to assemble. This binds nearly every later section. It would reopen if UKB's coverage of the owned domains became good enough to absorb the Top-50 set at the required accuracy, or if the owned corpus grew large enough that "controlled" stopped being tractable.

**Decision 2: Identity-derived scoping happens before retrieval, not after.** PAPI is not a downstream enrichment step; it is the step that produces the metadata needed to shape retrieval in the first place. If a caller supplied only an alias or employee identifier, Skywalker calls PAPI before any similarity matching, retrieval, or reranking. Only the direct-scope entry mode bypasses it. This binds the request lifecycle and the failure model (a request whose scope cannot be resolved cannot be safely answered). It would reopen if PAPI latency or availability made pre-retrieval resolution untenable and a cached or deferred model proved necessary.

**Decision 3: Routing is a hybrid gate against a static variant set.** Cosine similarity runs first; a Cohere Rerank v4 cross-encoder on a dedicated SageMaker endpoint runs second, but only when cosine is ambiguous. A cross-encoder reads the query and a candidate together and scores their relevance jointly, which is more accurate than comparing two independent vectors but too expensive to run on every case — hence the two-stage shape. If the gate is confident the query belongs to the Top-50 set, the request goes to the FAQ arm only. Otherwise the system does not abandon the FAQ arm; it queries both arms concurrently and lets them compete in one reranking stage. This binds the query pipeline ([04_Query_Pipeline_and_Online_Decision_Flow.md](04_Query_Pipeline_and_Online_Decision_Flow.md)). It would reopen if measured routing error in the near-miss region showed the gate either admitting too much noise or short-circuiting too aggressively.

**Decision 4: Concurrency eliminates sequential arm latency on the dual-arm path.** When both arms participate, they run at the same time, so the architecture pays for parallel work but not for serial fan-out latency. Cost is established as a non-issue for this program, so the governing reason to keep or change this later is answer quality, not compute spend. This binds the latency budget in Decision 9. It would reopen only if a downstream dependency made concurrent invocation unsafe.

**Decision 5: The reranker is the common scoring surface.** Arm-local scores are not treated as globally meaningful. UKB exposes its own notion of relevance; the FAQ arm has its own retrieval behavior; Skywalker does not try to reconcile those source scores into one synthetic number before reranking. It uses the reranker as the single place where heterogeneous candidates compete against the same query under one model. This binds candidate normalization and abstain behavior ([07_Reranking_Candidate_Unification_and_Abstain_Behavior.md](07_Reranking_Candidate_Unification_and_Abstain_Behavior.md)). It would reopen if the reranker proved unable to score cross-arm candidates fairly at the required accuracy.

**Decision 6: The FAQ arm is a retrieval system, not a cache of answer blobs.** The corpus is small and rebuilt daily on change, but the architecture still treats Top-50 handling as retrieval over a tightly controlled corpus rather than keyed lookup over authored answers. The routing gate decides whether a user's wording belongs to the Top-50 question set; retrieval over the owned corpus still does the evidence work inside the arm. This binds the ingestion design's choice to embed and index fragments rather than store canned responses. It would reopen if the question set became closed and stable enough that exact-match lookup outperformed retrieval.

**Decision 7: Skywalker is stateless per request.** Multi-turn continuity belongs to the model-facing agent layer. For Slack, the Slack-side agent and its model hold conversation history; for QuickSuite, QuickSuite's chat runtime does. Skywalker benefits from that work — a better agent asks a better-scoped follow-up query — but it stores and reasons over no conversation state itself. This binds the client boundary sections (08 and 09). It would reopen only if a retrieval-layer feature genuinely required cross-request memory, which the current design does not.

**Decision 8: The active correctness dimensions are geography, level, and employee class.** Three dimensions are sufficient for the current architecture, and the ingestion code enforces all three as required on every published fragment. If corpus reality later proves another identity dimension is needed, that is a re-litigation event, not the active design. It would reopen the moment a real content example demonstrates a correct answer that the three-dimension model cannot express.

**Decision 9: The latency budget is an architectural constraint.** The Skywalker retrieval pipeline — from MCP request acceptance to normalized evidence return — targets a p95 of 250 to 450 milliseconds. The reranker call on its dedicated SageMaker endpoint is the dominant cost in that budget, targeting 250–350 ms p95 on 20K-token payloads (see [research_grounding_docs/API_10_Rerankers.md](research_grounding_docs/API_10_Rerankers.md)); the endpoint instance type that meets this target is still being selected (Section 8), so this is the bar the chosen instance must clear, not a property of an instance already in place; the remaining ~100 ms covers scope resolution, the concurrent arm calls, candidate normalization, and post-rerank sibling expansion. End to end on the Slack surface, measured from user message receipt to final Slack reply, the target is under 4 seconds p95. These budgets are constraints, not calibration surfaces: they are why the dual-arm path uses concurrency rather than sequential fan-out, why the explicit-scope mode avoids a redundant PAPI round-trip, and why agent-side generation latency matters as much as retrieval latency on the Slack path. A sustained breach of either budget is an architecture-class event, not a tuning inconvenience.

**Decision 10: The launch configuration is the full dual-arm hybrid system.** Skywalker launches with both retrieval arms live — the owned FAQ arm and the UKB general arm competing through one reranker — rather than shipping a FAQ-only slice first and adding the general arm later. The FAQ-only configuration in [05_UAT_Frontend_and_FAQ_Only_Slice.md](05_UAT_Frontend_and_FAQ_Only_Slice.md) is a UAT and validation slice that exercises the owned path in isolation, not the launch product shape. This binds the client and query-pipeline sections to assume both arms are present from day one. It would reopen only if UKB integration readiness slipped against the launch date, in which case a FAQ-only launch becomes the fallback rather than the plan.

## 4. Alternatives considered

Several designs were genuinely attractive and were still rejected. Recording why is the difference between a decision and a guess.

**The pure UKB-only design** would have made Skywalker a thin wrapper around UKB with identity scoping layered on top. It is attractive because it is the least code to build and the fastest path to broad coverage. It was rejected because the program is most exposed on the Top-50 set, and UKB is a black box whose retrieval, chunking, ranking, and indexing internals Skywalker does not control. Relying on it for the exact questions where the team is judged most tightly would put the hardest part of the program in the place with the least control. That violates the controlled-accuracy tenet directly.

**Building the general arm ourselves** over the relevant travel, events, and expense documentation was the earlier plan, and it is attractive because it would have given us end-to-end control of both arms. It became less attractive once UKB was available as an MCP-accessible knowledge base already built on the retrieval substrate we would otherwise have had to assemble. The current design keeps ownership only where ownership buys the most: the Top-50 path.

**The always-query-both design** would send every request to both arms and let the reranker sort it out every time. It is attractive for its uniformity — one code path, no routing decision to get wrong. It is not the active design because a confident Top-50 match should short-circuit: the similarity stage exists not only to admit FAQ candidates into the race but to allow a genuine fast path when the request clearly belongs to the owned space. We keep the option live as a fallback shape if routing proves unreliable.

**The strict single-arm gate on every miss** would send any non-FAQ query to the general arm alone, skipping the FAQ arm entirely. It is attractive because it is the simplest routing rule. It was rejected because semantically adjacent Top-50 material can still be valuable to the reranker in the near-miss region, even when the system cannot justify FAQ-only routing. The current policy keeps the FAQ arm alive there on purpose.

**Server-side conversation state inside Skywalker** would let the backend track multi-turn meaning. It is superficially attractive because it would let Skywalker resolve ambiguous follow-ups itself. It was rejected because a deterministic retrieval system is the wrong place for multi-turn meaning, and building it would pull the backend toward agent behavior. The agents already handle conversation history well enough to own it.

**Aggressive PAPI caching inside Skywalker** would trade freshness for latency on scope resolution. It is attractive for the latency budget. It is not part of the active design because the current architecture favors clarity over extra machinery at that point in the request path; PAPI is simply the first dependency after the MCP boundary when scope must be resolved.

## 5. Assumptions inherited from the program

As the first section, this chapter inherits no technical detail from prior subsystem chapters, but it does inherit several program premises that later sections should treat as established unless explicitly revisited. Each is stated with its consequence if it proves false, because an unstated assumption is the one that breaks a build under pressure.

The domain is bounded: travel, events, and expense documentation, not all of Amazon. If that scope expands, the routing gate's variant set and the owned-corpus assumptions both need revisiting.

The owned FAQ corpus is small — effectively one document today, with a near-term upper bound on the order of ten. This is the premise that licenses daily polling and full rebuild on change instead of incremental mutation machinery. If the corpus grew by an order of magnitude, the rebuild model in [03_Top_50_FAQ_Controlled_Corpus_Ingestion_and_Storage.md](03_Top_50_FAQ_Controlled_Corpus_Ingestion_and_Storage.md) would need to be reconsidered.

COREx is the source of owned FAQ content and UKB is the source of broad general retrieval. They are different dependencies with different control surfaces, and later sections must not collapse them into one abstract "documentation source."

Slack and QuickSuite both provide an alias rather than a pre-resolved scope tuple. Slack supplies a Slack user ID that the bot resolves to an Amazon alias; QuickSuite supplies a Federate JWT whose `sub` claim is the Midway login. Both therefore enter through the alias mode and both require PAPI resolution before retrieval, unless a caller deliberately uses the explicit-scope short-circuit. The employee-ID and explicit-scope modes remain in the contract for any future caller holding those richer identifiers, even though neither launch client uses them by default.

Cost is not a design driver. That does not make waste a virtue; it means correctness, controllability, and quality take precedence over optimization pressure that would otherwise push the architecture toward a thinner retrieval path.

## 6. End-to-end request lifecycle

The lifecycle below is intentionally end-to-end at the orchestration level. Later sections explode individual steps into detail, but the boundary-setting version has to be visible now so that later detail stays attached to one coherent request story.

The flow begins when an external caller invokes Skywalker's MCP server through Amazon MCP Gateway. The caller is one of: the Slack inline-agent orchestrator (CloudAuth inbound on `/ca/mcp/...`, human alias propagated via TransitiveAuth); the UAT inline-agent orchestrator (CloudAuth inbound, human alias via TransitiveAuth, scope triple supplied as MCP tool arguments); QuickSuite's chat runtime (Federate OAuth inbound on `/federate/mcp/...`, identity in MCP tool arguments); or another approved future caller. The incoming request carries a user query and one of the supported identity entry modes, with identity material arriving on the per-route channel — TransitiveAuth claims on the CloudAuth paths, MCP tool arguments on the Federate path. At this point the request is still caller-shaped, and Skywalker's first job is to make it retrieval-shaped.

If the caller supplied only an alias or employee identifier, Skywalker calls PAPI immediately and receives the scoped identity metadata retrieval needs: geography, level, and employee class. If the caller supplied resolved scope directly, Skywalker skips this step and treats the supplied scope as authoritative for the request. Either way, retrieval does not begin until the scoping triple is attached.

Once scope exists, Skywalker runs the hybrid routing gate against the in-memory variant set, which is loaded from S3 at service boot (see [research_grounding_docs/API_11_S3_Variant_Set.md](research_grounding_docs/API_11_S3_Variant_Set.md)): cosine similarity first, with the Cohere Rerank v4 cross-encoder on the gate's dedicated SageMaker endpoint only when cosine is ambiguous. This is the first routing decision in the live path, and its verdict is neither the answer nor the final confidence signal — it is a routing signal driven by control-plane thresholds held in SSM.

If the verdict is strong enough for FAQ-only routing, Skywalker sends the request only to the owned FAQ arm, which retrieves candidates from the Top-50 corpus using the resolved scope. That arm's output never passes straight through to the caller; it is converted into the common candidate form the reranker expects.

If the verdict does not clear the FAQ-only threshold, Skywalker fans out to both arms concurrently. The FAQ arm receives the query and scope. The UKB arm receives the query and the same resolved scope through its own request shape. Both return candidate evidence, Skywalker normalizes both sets into one representation, and merges them into a single reranker input pool.

The reranker then scores the normalized candidates against the query as one competition set. From that point the system no longer cares which arm a candidate came from except as route metadata; what matters is which candidate best answers the query for the scoped user.

After reranking, Skywalker makes the answerability decision. If no candidate clears the bottom answerability bar, Skywalker abstains — it returns a structured "no answer" result rather than overclaiming. If one or more candidates clear the bar, Skywalker returns the ranked evidence bundle with route metadata and source metadata.

The final step happens outside Skywalker. The caller-side agent turns the structured retrieval output into the user-facing response, including conversational context, follow-up handling, and surface-specific answer style. Those behaviors are downstream of Skywalker and must not be confused with the retrieval backend.

The runtime path summarizes to one deterministic progression: caller request enters MCP; scope is resolved; the routing gate runs; one arm or both arms retrieve; candidates are normalized; the reranker chooses across the combined set; Skywalker returns a structured bundle or abstains; the agent consumes that output and owns the conversational layer.

## 7. Failure behavior, abstain behavior, and the non-goals that protect the boundary

Failure behavior at the boundary level preserves one invariant above all: answers are scoped correctly or they are not returned as though they were safe. Failure handling is specified here in terms of what the caller sees and whether the system fails open or closed, because a system's quality is defined by its failure behavior more than its success behavior.

**Scoping fails closed.** If a request arrives without direct scope and PAPI cannot resolve the identity retrieval needs, Skywalker does not continue into retrieval as though a generic answer were acceptable. In this architecture it is not. The system either receives the scoping triple directly or obtains it through PAPI; if neither happens, the request cannot be safely answered, and the caller receives a structured failure rather than an unscoped result.

**The dual-arm path degrades to single-arm.** If the request is on the dual-arm path and one arm fails while the other returns candidates, Skywalker continues with the surviving arm and marks the route accordingly in the structured output. The reranker still has a coherent, if narrower, candidate pool. And if the request was on the FAQ-only route and the FAQ arm fails, the safer fallback is to attempt the general arm rather than convert an arm outage into a guaranteed no-answer. Routing is a quality policy; it is not a reason to waste a viable fallback.

**Reranker failure is a fallback event, not total failure.** The boundary contract allows Skywalker to return the best available normalized retrieval set in retrieval order, flagged as a reranker-failure fallback, when reranking is unavailable. How much of that fallback set is allowed through is decided in [07_Reranking_Candidate_Unification_and_Abstain_Behavior.md](07_Reranking_Candidate_Unification_and_Abstain_Behavior.md); the architecture does not assume the only two states are "reranked perfectly" and "nothing returned."

**Abstention is distinct from component failure.** A request can execute successfully and still produce no answerable evidence. That is not an outage; it is the system doing the correct thing when it cannot defend a grounded answer. Skywalker distinguishes the two cleanly: in one case the path broke, and in the other the path ran and declined to overclaim. The structured response carries that distinction so the agent can word the two situations differently.

The non-goals that protect this boundary are worth restating, because they are what keep later sections honest. Skywalker does not own conversational memory, prompt engineering for Slack or QuickSuite, or the final wording shown to users. It does not own the evaluation harness. It does not own human-in-the-loop runtime review, even though subject-matter-expert review after launch is a real and important program practice. That review matters; it is simply not part of Skywalker's runtime ownership boundary, and keeping it outside the boundary is what lets the runtime stay deterministic and testable.

## 8. Calibration surfaces

Three classes of setting are deliberately distinct in this architecture, and conflating them is how an architecture review goes wrong. Fixed decisions (Section 3) are architecture-class: changing one invalidates other subsystems. Calibration-active settings are intentionally empirical — given a reasoned starting value and tuned against real data. Open decisions (Section 9) are choices the architecture has deliberately not yet made. This section names the calibration-active settings that live at the boundary; per-subsystem calibration detail belongs to each later section, and the consolidated calibration-and-decision log is [10_Calibration_Relitigation_Triggers_and_Decision_Log.md](10_Calibration_Relitigation_Triggers_and_Decision_Log.md).

The routing gate's thresholds are calibration-active. The cosine score above which a query is treated as confidently Top-50, and the ambiguity band within which the Cohere Rerank v4 cross-encoder second stage runs, both start from reasoned defaults held in SSM and move as observed routing behavior demands. They live in the control plane precisely so they can change without a redeploy.

The abstain bar is calibration-active. The answerability floor below which Skywalker returns "no grounded answer" rather than a result is empirical: set it too high and the system abstains on answerable questions, too low and it overclaims. It starts conservative and is tuned against real query traffic.

The reranker hosting instance is calibration-active and additionally gated on executive approval. The reranker endpoint dominates the latency budget, and the right instance is a cost-versus-latency choice that is not yet fixed. Three candidates are under evaluation — `ml.g5.xlarge`, `ml.g5.2xlarge`, and `ml.p5.4xlarge` — and choosing among them is a latency measurement against the Decision 9 target, not an architectural decision. Provisioning any of them is also a financial commitment: an always-on SageMaker endpoint for this model class carries a flat per-hour software fee on top of the instance, which puts the annual cost in the band that requires both L6 manager and L6 finance approval. The instance choice is therefore pending two things — the measurement that identifies the right cost-versus-latency fit, and that L6 approval — so the latency targets in Decision 9 are the bar the chosen instance must clear, stated independently of which instance wins.

## 9. Open decisions

Most of the choices this series leaves open are open on purpose, and they share one reason: they are answerable only with real usage. The owned corpus is small and its question distribution is, at this stage, an educated guess. The routing thresholds, the abstain bar, the near-miss dual-arm policy, and the relative value of FAQ versus UKB candidates are all questions whose right answers depend on what real employees actually ask and how the system actually scores their queries. Tuning them against synthetic data would be calibrating to a fiction. The architecture therefore fixes the structure now and defers the empirical settings to first contact with production traffic — concretely, the few-hundred-user scale the September launch is expected to reach. Each open decision below names the evidence that will close it.

The reranker hosting instance is open pending the cost-versus-latency measurement and the L6 approval described in Section 8. It closes when the endpoint is provisioned on the chosen instance.

The near-miss routing boundary is open. The policy is fixed to dual-arm in that region (Decision 3), but where exactly "confident Top-50" ends, "near miss" begins, and "clearly general" takes over is not.
