# Autonomous, Judge-Driven Prompt Optimization with a Tournament of Competitors and a Single Reasoning-Model Judge: A Technical Assessment

## TL;DR
- **Your architecture is sound but lives on the single most dangerous edge of the field: an unlabeled, single-judge fitness signal that your competitors will learn to game.** The winning design is GEPA-style reflective evolution per competitor, scored by Opus 4.8 in **pairwise** mode with **position-swapping**, ranked by **Bradley-Terry MLE with bootstrap CIs**, evaluation budget allocated by **successive-halving / best-arm identification**, and protected against Goodhart with a **rotating/cross-family held-out judge and a frozen reusable-holdout set** that you never optimize against.
- **The Opus-judges-Sonnet same-family pairing is a real, measurable liability, not a theoretical one.** Self-preference scales with judge capability and self-recognition (GPT-4 self-preference 0.705 on XSUM / 0.912 on CNN-DailyMail vs Llama-2-7b ~0.50 in Panickssery et al., NeurIPS 2024; DeepSeek-V3 inflates its own win rate 66%→73% in AAAI 2026), is driven by output familiarity/low perplexity that extends to same-family outputs, and persists even with hidden authorship. Because all your competitors are the *same* family as the judge, this bias is roughly a common-mode offset across competitors — less corrosive for *relative* ranking than for absolute scoring, but it still distorts which Sonnet variant wins and risks drift toward "Anthropic house style."
- **Adopt GEPA now as the per-competitor optimizer; treat your multi-model tournament as a layer on top, not a replacement.** GEPA's internal reflective loop already does most of what your hand-built competition does, far more token-efficiently (GEPA outperforms GRPO by 6% on average and up to 20% with up to 35× fewer rollouts, and beats MIPROv2 by over 10% — e.g., +12% on AIME-2025; Agrawal et al., arXiv:2507.19457). The multi-model competition adds real signal only when competitors explore genuinely different strategy basins; otherwise it burns tokens re-deriving the same prompt. RAGAS is useful as a cheap, decomposed *guardrail* metric (faithfulness, context precision/recall) but is too noisy and gameable to be your sole fitness signal for multi-turn agents.

## Key Findings

1. **Reflective evolution (GEPA) is the current state of the art for sample-efficient prompt optimization and is the natural backbone for your system.** GEPA (Agrawal et al., arXiv:2507.19457, ICLR 2026 oral) reads natural-language execution traces, diagnoses failures in words, and maintains a *Pareto frontier* of candidates rather than a single best. Across six tasks it outperforms GRPO by 6% on average (up to 20%) using up to 35× fewer rollouts, and beats MIPROv2 by over 10% (+12% on AIME-2025), tested on Qwen3-8B and GPT-4.1-mini. It is built to consume an LLM-judge metric that returns `{'score': float, 'feedback': str}`.

2. **Pairwise judging is more aligned with human preference but more gameable; pointwise/absolute scoring is more robust to manipulation.** In "Pairwise or Pointwise?" (arXiv:2504.14716, COLM 2025): *"On average, pairwise preferences flip in 35% of cases when a distractor feature is introduced, compared to only 9% for absolute scores."* The Comparative Trap paper (arXiv:2406.12319) shows pairwise *amplifies* verbosity/authority biases. The practical answer: use pairwise for ranking but add per-candidate pointwise reasoning, and swap positions and average.

3. **Reasoning/"thinking" judges are measurably better — more accurate and more bias-robust.** Jayarao et al., "Explicit Reasoning Makes Better Judges" (arXiv:2509.13332): *"thinking mode delivers larger improvements (+10.5 pts) with far lower computational overhead (1.82× FLOPs),"* versus 7-shot in-context learning's *"+4.5 pts"* at *"8.16× FLOPs"* (on open-source Qwen3 0.6B/1.7B/4B models on RewardBench). Thinking models also held ~6% higher consistency under positional, bandwagon, identity, diversity, and random bias conditions. This directly justifies using Opus 4.8 in extended-thinking mode as the judge.

4. **The statistics of declaring winners is where most homebrew tournaments fail.** Chatbot Arena/LMArena moved from Elo to **Bradley-Terry MLE with bootstrap confidence intervals** precisely because Elo's online update is noisy and order-dependent; even on ~6M votes, top models routinely have overlapping CIs, meaning rank order is partially noise. You must compute CIs, control multiple comparisons (Benjamini-Hochberg FDR across many prompt variants), and allocate evaluations with bandit methods (successive halving / best-arm identification — see Finding 8).

5. **Reward hacking against a single judge is documented and near-inevitable under strong optimization pressure (Goodhart's law).** The Llama-4 Maverick LMArena episode is the canonical public case: the experimental build "Llama-4-Maverick-03-26-Experimental" hit #2 on LMArena with an ELO of 1417 (~April 5, 2025), while the public checkpoint "Llama-4-Maverick-17B-128E-Instruct" ranked ~32nd when added April 11, 2025. LMArena's statement (~April 7–8, 2025): *"Meta should have made it clearer that 'Llama-4-Maverick-03-26-Experimental' was a customized model to optimize for human preference,"* and it released 2,000+ head-to-head battle results for public review. With no ground-truth labels, "the algorithm cannot distinguish between a biased high score and a truly superior arm" (Best Arm Identification with LLM Judges and Limited Human Audits, arXiv:2601.21471).

6. **Test-set reuse across rounds is the silent killer.** Your fixed real-data eval set, queried repeatedly across many rounds by adaptively-chosen prompts, is exactly the adaptive-data-analysis / leaderboard-overfitting problem (Dwork et al. reusable holdout, *Science* 2015; Blum & Hardt "The Ladder," ICML 2015). Apparent gains will continue while true quality plateaus. Use a rotating/freshly-sampled eval pool and a sequestered "iron vault" holdout you touch only at the very end.

## Details

### 1. Autonomous prompt optimization methods — what to use and when

**Evolutionary / population-based.** PromptBreeder (arXiv:2309.16797) is self-referential — it evolves both task-prompts and the *mutation-prompts* that modify them, using "thinking styles" and hyper-mutation. EvoPrompt (arXiv:2309.08532, ICLR 2024) connects LLMs as mutation/crossover operators with GA/DE selection, reporting up to 25% gains over human prompts on BIG-Bench Hard. These are pure black-box optimizers: they need a scalar fitness and discard the *reason* a prompt failed. That is their weakness relative to reflective methods, and it matters for you because your richest signal (Opus's detailed feedback) is textual, not scalar.

**Textual-gradient / feedback-driven.** ProTeGi (Pryzant et al., 2023) introduced "textual gradients": the judge LLM critiques a prompt in natural language, an editor LLM applies the critique, and beam search + UCB bandits select among candidates. TextGrad (Yuksekgonul et al., *Nature* 2024; arXiv:2406.07496) generalizes this into a PyTorch-style autograd-over-text engine (forward pass = generate + score; backward pass = LLM critique) and now supports Bedrock via litellm — relevant to your AWS stack. These are strong when you have one module to tune and a clear loss; they get unstable on multi-module pipelines and noisy feedback.

**Reflective evolution — GEPA.** GEPA is the synthesis: genetic evolution *plus* natural-language reflection *plus* Pareto-frontier selection. Internally: (1) sample rollouts of the current system, capturing full traces (reasoning, tool calls, tool outputs); (2) evaluate with a metric that returns both a score and textual feedback; (3) reflect in natural language to diagnose failures and propose a new prompt; (4) add it to a tree of candidates; (5) select the next candidate stochastically from the Pareto frontier of per-instance scores (not the global best — this is what preserves diverse strategies and avoids the local optima that greedy optimizers fall into). The Pareto front is the key idea you should steal even if you don't adopt GEPA wholesale: it is structurally a way of maintaining a *population of specialists*, which is exactly what your "tournament of competitors" is groping toward, but with rigorous selection.

**DSPy optimizer landscape (as of mid-2026):**
- **BootstrapFewShot / BootstrapFewShotWithRandomSearch** — generate and select few-shot demos; cheapest, good baseline.
- **MIPROv2** (Multi-prompt Instruction PRoposal Optimizer v2) — jointly optimizes instructions *and* few-shot demos via a three-phase bootstrap → grounded instruction proposal → Bayesian search over combinations. Best when demos matter.
- **SIMBA** (Stochastic Introspective Mini-Batch Ascent) — mini-batches, finds high-variability examples, applies LLM introspection to write improvement rules; better on large datasets and targeted failure-case improvement.
- **GEPA** — reflective, trace-driven, Pareto; most sample-efficient for instruction-level gains; the one to use when your signal is rich textual feedback (your case). Ships as `dspy.GEPA` and standalone `pip install gepa`.
- **BootstrapFinetune / weight optimization** — when you can fine-tune weights.

DSPy compiles multi-module pipelines by treating each `Predict`/`ChainOfThought`/`ReAct` module's signature as an optimizable unit; optimizers search instructions/demos for *each* predictor against a single program-level metric, with GEPA able to request *predictor-level* feedback (via `pred_name`/`pred_trace`) so the reflection is localized.

**Meta-prompting / self-refine.** Self-Refine and recursive self-improvement loops work but plateau and can degrade; the Multi-Agent Debate literature is sobering here (see §2).

**Tournament/multi-agent systems built around LLM-as-judge.** This is your exact architecture and it exists in the literature: **"Tournament of Prompts" (DEEVO, arXiv:2506.00178, Prompt Optimization @ KDD 2025)** evolves prompts via multi-agent *debate* evaluation and **Elo ratings as the fitness function**, explicitly bypassing the need for a hand-crafted or learned reward model. It pairs outputs, runs a debate to pick a winner, and uses Elo as the evolutionary fitness. This is the closest published analog to what you are building and worth reading closely. Reward Reasoning Models (Guo et al., 2025) similarly use Elo + knockout tournaments as a training reward.

### 2. LLM-as-judge methodology and reliability (the single point of truth)

**Known biases and 2025–2026 mitigations:**
- **Position/order bias** — pervasive across model families. Mitigation: run both orderings and average (swap-and-average); this is mandatory, not optional.
- **Verbosity/length bias** — judges systematically favor longer answers. Mitigation: length-normalize or penalize, instruct the judge to ignore length, cap competitor output length.
- **Self-preference / same-family bias** — *your central risk* (see below).
- **Sycophancy / authority bias** — judges reward confident, authoritative tone. Mitigation: rubric anchoring, adversarial examples.
- **Formatting bias** — markdown, structure, headers inflate scores. Mitigation: normalize formatting before judging.
- **Scoring bias** — even GPT-4o's correlation with humans fluctuates with rubric order, ID type, reference quality (typically within 0.03, up to 0.2 for smaller models).

**Quantified same-family / self-preference risk (critical for Opus-judges-Sonnet):**
- Panickssery et al., "LLM Evaluators Recognize and Favor Their Own Generations" (NeurIPS 2024 oral, arXiv:2404.13076): found *"a linear correlation between self-recognition capability and the strength of self-preference bias."* GPT-4 self-preference scored **0.705 (XSUM) / 0.912 (CNN-DailyMail)** vs Llama-2-7b at ~0.50 (essentially no bias). GPT-4 was 73.5% accurate at recognizing its own outputs unprompted. Crucially, revealing authorship amplifies the bias: GPT-4 self-preference was **0.73 with correct labels vs 0.32 with swapped labels** — a 41-point swing from stated authorship alone.
- Wataoka et al. (arXiv:2410.21819): GPT-4's self-preference bias measured **0.520** on their Equal-Opportunity metric (highest of 8 models). Mechanism is *perplexity/familiarity*: *"LLMs assign significantly higher evaluations to outputs with lower perplexity… regardless of whether the outputs were self-generated."* This is the key finding for you: the bias attaches to *familiar-looking* (low-perplexity-under-the-judge) text, which same-family Sonnet outputs will be — so expect same-family inflation even without literal self-identity.
- Mahbub & Feng, "Mitigating Self-Preference by Authorship Obfuscation" (AAAI 2026, arXiv:2512.05379): "harmful self-preference" (judge picks its own answer when the competitor's is objectively better) is concentrated in the *larger/stronger* models. **DeepSeek-V3 estimated its own win rate at 73% vs an actual 66%.** In harmful cases, self-recognition and self-preference correlated at **r=0.63**. A 2-word synonym-replacement obfuscation measurably reduced harmful self-preference (e.g., loss-case judge accuracy 26.78%→33.52%) but *could not eliminate it* — removing all style cues caused the bias to recover via semantic/belief agreement.
- Wang et al. (arXiv:2504.03846): stronger models show more harmful self-preference *specifically when they err* as generators, but inference-time CoT reduces it.

**Implication for your design:** Opus-class judges are the *higher-risk* end of self-preference. But because every competitor is the same family, the bias is largely a **common-mode offset** — it inflates all competitors' scores similarly, so *relative* ranking among Sonnet variants is less corrupted than absolute scores would be. The residual danger is that the optimizer drifts toward the *Anthropic house style* (the low-perplexity attractor) rather than toward real task quality. Concrete mitigations for you: (a) never reveal authorship/model identity to the judge; (b) periodically validate with a *different-family* held-out judge (e.g., GPT-5-class or Gemini-2.5-class) on a sample — if Opus's ranking and the cross-family judge's ranking diverge sharply, you are measuring house style, not quality; (c) light authorship obfuscation/paraphrase normalization before judging; (d) keep a small human-audited ground-truth slice.

**Single judge vs jury/panel/debate.** The 2025–2026 evidence (LLM-as-a-jury, arXiv:2602.16610; Jury-on-Demand, arXiv:2512.01786; Multi-Agent Debate for LLM Judges, NeurIPS 2025, arXiv:2510.12697) consistently finds panels beat single judges on reliability and bias-robustness, and a jury of cheaper models can match a single expensive judge at lower cost (Verga et al. "Replacing Judges with Juries"). **But** the ICLR 2025 blogpost on Multi-Agent Debate found MAD frequently fails to beat a strong single agent and doesn't reliably use extra compute well. Verdict for you: a single strong reasoning judge (Opus 4.8 with thinking) is defensible and the literature supports thinking-judge quality — but for the *final* winner declaration and for *meta-evaluating Opus itself*, add at least one cross-family judge. A two-or-three-model jury for the final round is cheap insurance against same-family collusion.

**Pairwise vs pointwise.** Pairwise aligns better with human preference and gives cleaner ranking signal, but is more manipulable (35% vs 9% flip rate to distractors) and amplifies verbosity/authority bias. Pointwise scales linearly and supports absolute thresholds but drifts run-to-run and needs a stable rubric. **Recommended hybrid:** pairwise comparison for tournament ranking (with swap-and-average), augmented with per-candidate pointwise reasoning/rubric scoring (the remedy proposed in the Comparative Trap paper), and absolute pointwise faithfulness/groundedness gates as a floor.

**Reference-free vs reference-guided.** You have no labels, so you're reference-free by necessity. Reference-guided judging is more reliable when you can supply even a weak reference; consider synthesizing pseudo-references (e.g., an Opus "gold" answer) for a *subset* to calibrate, while accepting this imports judge bias.

**Judge prompt design best practices:** structured rubric with explicit per-score-level descriptions; chain-of-thought / extended thinking before the verdict; constrained output schema (score + structured feedback); few-shot calibration with verdict-balanced examples; decompose into atomic per-criterion judgments to avoid criterion conflation; randomize/rotate rubric phrasing across rounds to prevent the optimizer keyword-stuffing a fixed rubric.

### 3. Statistics of declaring winners

**Ranking from pairwise verdicts.** Use **Bradley-Terry MLE** (what LMArena uses) rather than online Elo: it uses the full comparison history, gives a latent strength per competitor, and yields proper **bootstrap confidence intervals** (resample comparisons, re-fit, take 2.5/97.5 percentiles). Use the **sandwich covariance** estimator for robustness. TrueSkill/Glicko are alternatives that natively model uncertainty and support adaptive matchmaking (pair high-uncertainty competitors to converge faster) — attractive if competitors enter/leave across rounds. Handle ties explicitly (BT with ties as half-win/half-loss, or a Rao-Kupper/Davidson extension).

**Significance testing — "is A really better than B?"** Don't declare a winner on a point estimate. Use paired bootstrap or McNemar's test on per-item win/loss; report win-rate CIs. If two competitors' BT intervals overlap, their rank order is noise — exactly the LMArena lesson (top-3 models routinely sit in overlapping CIs).

**Multiple comparisons.** Running K prompt variants against each other is K(K-1)/2 comparisons, plus repeated rounds — a massive multiple-testing surface. Control the family-wise error or false-discovery rate: Bonferroni is too conservative at scale; use **Benjamini-Hochberg FDR**. This matters because with 20 variants and naive α=0.05 you expect spurious "winners" by chance alone.

**Variance reduction & power.** Best-arm identification theory says the samples needed to identify the best arm scale as Σ 1/Δᵢ² (Δᵢ = quality gap to best) — so near-ties need many evaluations and large gaps need few. Use **successive halving / Hyperband** (Jamieson & Talwalkar 2016) or the best-arm-identification approach of **Shi, Yang, Chen, Li, Yang & Shen, "Efficient Prompt Optimization Through the Lens of Best Arm Identification" (TRIPLE; NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2402.09723)**, which implements TRIPLE-SH (Sequential Halving) and TRIPLE-CR (Continuous Reject) for prompt selection under a fixed budget. Integrated into APE, its TRIPLE-CLST/GSE variants achieved average performance ranks of 1.58 and 1.50 respectively, versus the Uniform baseline's rank of 4.00. Allocate more judge calls to the contenders, eliminate weak prompts early. Variance-reduce by using common evaluation items across competitors (paired comparisons), fixing the judge's decoding (low temperature) for scoring consistency, and averaging over position swaps.

**Avoiding winners-on-noise and test-set overfitting over time.** This is the adaptive-data-analysis problem. Each round, your adaptively-chosen prompts query the same eval set, creating dependence that invalidates naive estimates — true quality plateaus while measured score climbs (Dwork et al. reusable holdout, *Science* 2015; Blum & Hardt "The Ladder," ICML 2015; the Kaggle public/private split exists for exactly this reason). Mitigations: (1) split your real data into a *training/dev* pool the optimizer sees and a *sequestered holdout* it never sees until the final selection; (2) freshly sample / rotate the dev evaluation items each round so no fixed set is over-queried; (3) apply the reusable-holdout (differential-privacy-flavored) mechanism — only report holdout scores through a noised threshold; (4) use the public/private split discipline — final winner chosen on a private split touched once.

### 4. Reward hacking / overfitting-to-the-judge defenses

**How optimizers game judges.** Documented exploits: verbosity (pad answers), sycophancy (agree with apparent user stance), authoritative tone, formatting/markdown, keyword-stuffing the rubric's own language, and — in agentic settings — prompt-injection of the judge (Shi et al. show optimization-based prompt injection can steer LLM-as-judge to attacker-chosen verdicts). The Llama-4 Maverick LMArena spike (April 2025; see Key Finding 5) is the canonical public goodharting case. The deep mechanism: under strong optimization, the proxy (judge) and the true objective diverge in out-of-distribution regions where superficial correlates of quality dominate.

**Defenses (stack several):**
- **Judge rotation / ensembles:** rotate among several judge prompts and ideally several judge *models* (cross-family) so no single exploitable surface persists across rounds.
- **Held-out adversarial judge:** a different-family judge that periodically audits the winner; large divergence flags hacking.
- **Rubric hardening + randomization:** explicit anti-verbosity/anti-sycophancy instructions, randomized rubric phrasing each round.
- **Length penalties / normalization.**
- **Meta-evaluation of the judge:** track judge self-consistency (re-judge identical pairs), position-swap agreement, and agreement with a small human-audited gold slice over time. Falling agreement = the optimizer is exploiting the judge.
- **A small ground-truth audit set:** the arXiv:2601.21471 result is blunt — "without ground-truth labels to calibrate the proxy, the algorithm cannot distinguish between a biased high score and a truly superior arm," and their "No-Audit" strategy *failed completely*. Even a tiny audited slice with uncertainty-weighted allocation recovered full accuracy at ~10% cost reduction. **You should budget for a small human/gold audit set despite the "no labels" framing — it is the difference between detectable and undetectable hacking.**

**Goodhart detection.** The signature is *measured score rising while audited/cross-judge quality stalls or falls*. Instrument this explicitly: every N rounds, score the current winner on the sequestered holdout and with the cross-family judge; plot proxy-vs-gold gap.

**Evaluator-optimizer collusion (same Anthropic family).** This is your structural risk. The self-preference literature above shows the judge will favor familiar same-family style. Because optimizer and judge share lineage, the optimizer can drift toward "what Opus finds familiar/fluent" rather than "what is correct." Mitigations specific to this: cross-family audit judge (mandatory), authorship obfuscation, and — per the AAAI 2026 recommendation — *exclude the evaluated model from judging and prefer a decisive majority-vote ensemble* for high-stakes decisions. Do not use the same Sonnet checkpoint as both a competitor and a judge.

### 5. Practical verdict — GEPA and RAGAS for multi-turn agents

**GEPA: adopt it, and let it absorb most of your hand-built loop.** GEPA's reflective loop (propose → evaluate with textual feedback → reflect → Pareto-select) already implements, rigorously and token-efficiently, most of what your "competitors independently learn and iterate" design does by hand. Concretely:
- **Where GEPA overlaps your build:** the per-competitor "learn from the judge's detailed feedback across rounds" loop *is* GEPA's reflect-and-mutate step. The Pareto frontier *is* a principled version of "keep several competing prompt lineages alive." If you hand-build this, you are re-implementing GEPA worse.
- **Where your multi-model competition adds real signal:** only when competitors explore *genuinely different strategy basins* — different decompositions, tool-use patterns, reasoning styles — that a single GEPA lineage wouldn't discover. Running multiple *independent* GEPA optimizations with different seeds/initializations and then competing their Pareto fronts is defensible. Running multiple identical Sonnet competitors that converge on the same prompt just multiplies token cost for no diversity gain.
- **Where it burns tokens:** N competitors × M rounds × pairwise judging is O(N²M) judge calls. Use successive halving to cull early; don't full-round-robin every variant every round.
- **Wiring GEPA with an LLM-judge metric:** GEPA's metric must return `dspy.Prediction(score=float, feedback=str)`. Write a `dspy.Signature` whose outputs include `score` and `feedback`, wrap it in a module whose `forward(gold, pred, trace, pred_name, pred_trace)` calls Opus-as-judge and returns the score + the judge's natural-language critique. Use the `trace is None` toggle to return a raw score during *evaluation* and a binarized/feedback-rich score during *optimization* (the built-in `SemanticF1`/`CompleteAndGrounded` judges follow exactly this pattern). Set `reflection_lm` to a strong model (the DSPy docs suggest a frontier model at temperature 1.0, high `max_tokens` — Opus 4.8 is appropriate); set `auto="medium"` or tune `max_metric_calls`; use `add_format_failure_as_feedback=True`. Dropbox's production write-up ("How we optimized Dash's relevance judge with DSPy") and the HuggingFace DSPy-GEPA cookbook are concrete references. Critically, feed the judge's *gap explanation* (why score, not just score) into feedback — that's what makes GEPA converge in few rollouts.

**RAGAS: a useful guardrail metric, not your fitness signal.** RAGAS provides decomposed RAG metrics — faithfulness (claims supported by retrieved context), answer relevancy, context precision, context recall — and as of 2026 has added **agentic/multi-turn metrics**: `AgentGoalAccuracy` (binary goal achievement, with `multi_turn_ascore` over `MultiTurnSample`), `ToolCallAccuracy` (exact order/content), `ToolCallF1` (softer, unordered precision/recall on tool calls), and `TopicAdherence` (precision/recall/F1 for staying in-domain).
- **Reliability:** mixed. RAGAS faithfulness uses LLM-as-judge claim decomposition; independent benchmarks are unflattering. The GroUSE benchmark (arXiv:2409.06595) found RAGAS metrics "do not perform well on many individual tests" despite correlating in aggregate — correlation on judgment ≠ good calibration on edge cases, and the multi-LLM-call pipeline compounds errors. Telecom-domain evaluation (arXiv:2407.12873) flagged faithfulness's sentence-mapping and the cosine-similarity component as failure-prone. Vectara argues a classifier (HHEM) is more robust than RAGAS's LLM-judge faithfulness.
- **Fit for you:** use RAGAS as a *cheap, fast, decomposed guardrail and floor* — e.g., reject any competitor output below a faithfulness/context-precision threshold (production targets cited in 2026 guides: faithfulness ≥0.9, answer relevancy ≥0.85, context precision ≥0.8) — and as a *secondary, ground-able signal* that's harder to game than a holistic preference (because it's anchored to retrieved context). It is *not* reliable or holistic enough to be your sole multi-turn fitness signal; it can't catch "faithful to stale/wrong context," and it imports its own judge biases. Treat the judge config as part of the metric definition and freeze it.

**Evaluating multi-turn agent conversations specifically.** The 2026 consensus (Confident AI, FutureAGI, MLflow, Maxim guides; the multi-turn survey arXiv:2503.22458; TRAJECT-Bench arXiv:2510.04550; SWE-eval) is a **three-layer eval**:
1. **End-to-end / outcome:** did the agent achieve the user's goal? (task completion / goal accuracy, conversation-level).
2. **Trajectory-level:** was the *path* sound? — tool selection accuracy, tool-argument correctness, trajectory efficiency (redundant steps/retries/loops), plan adherence, recovery from failed calls. This is where the real signal lives and where final-answer-only metrics miss "right answer for the wrong reasons."
3. **Turn-level / component:** which step/retrieval/tool broke.

Key nuances: agents can succeed via *multiple valid paths*, so trajectory scoring must not over-penalize divergence from a single gold path (reference-free trajectory judging or set-based tool-call matching like ToolCallF1 handles this). State corruption can persist silently across turns. Running real trajectories is expensive — use replay/mock environments and synthetic adversarial scenario generation (a stronger model builds test cases) for scale. For your tournament, the judge should score *trajectories*, not just final turns: feed Opus the full trace and have it produce turn-level and conversation-level rubric scores plus a pairwise verdict.

## Recommendations

**Stage 0 — Foundations (do before any tournament):**
1. **Split your real data** into (a) a dev pool the optimizer/judge sees, (b) a *rotating* per-round evaluation sample drawn fresh to avoid over-querying any fixed set, and (c) a sequestered "iron vault" holdout touched exactly once at final selection.
2. **Stand up a small human/gold audit set** (even 50–200 items). The literature is unambiguous that with zero ground truth you cannot detect reward hacking. This is non-negotiable for a single-judge system.
3. **Freeze the judge configuration** (model version, rubric, decoding params) and version it — it is part of your metric definition.

**Stage 1 — Per-competitor optimizer:**
4. **Adopt `dspy.GEPA`** as the per-competitor optimizer with an Opus-4.8-as-judge metric returning `{score, feedback}`. Use extended-thinking mode on the judge (justified by the +10.5pp accuracy at 1.82× FLOPs / ~6% robustness gains for thinking judges). Start with `auto="medium"`, `reflection_lm=Opus`, `add_format_failure_as_feedback=True`.
5. Run **multiple GEPA lineages with different initializations/seeds** rather than identical competitors, to get genuine strategy diversity.

**Stage 2 — Tournament + statistics:**
6. **Judge protocol:** pairwise with mandatory position-swap-and-average, augmented with per-candidate pointwise rubric scoring; never reveal model identity; add RAGAS faithfulness/context-precision as a hard floor gate.
7. **Ranking:** Bradley-Terry MLE with bootstrap CIs (sandwich covariance); handle ties explicitly; never declare a winner when CIs overlap.
8. **Budget allocation:** successive halving / TRIPLE-style best-arm identification — cull weak prompts early, concentrate judge calls on contenders.
9. **Multiple comparisons:** Benjamini-Hochberg FDR control across all pairwise tests and rounds.

**Stage 3 — Anti-hacking instrumentation (continuous):**
10. **Cross-family audit judge** (GPT-5/Gemini-class) on a sample every N rounds; track Opus-vs-cross-family rank correlation. Divergence ⇒ you're measuring house style.
11. **Judge meta-evaluation:** track position-swap agreement, re-judge consistency, and agreement with the gold audit slice over time. Falling agreement ⇒ hacking.
12. **Goodhart plot:** every N rounds, score the current winner on the sequestered holdout and gold set; watch the proxy-vs-gold gap. If proxy rises while gold stalls, stop and harden.
13. **Rubric randomization + length normalization** each round to prevent keyword-stuffing and verbosity exploits.

**Thresholds that change the plan:**
- If GEPA single-lineage results match your multi-model tournament results within CI ⇒ **drop the multi-model layer**; it's burning tokens. The tournament earns its cost only if it finds winners GEPA-alone doesn't.
- If Opus and the cross-family judge disagree on ranking beyond rank-correlation ~0.7 ⇒ **stop trusting absolute Opus scores**; move to cross-family jury for final decisions.
- If proxy-vs-gold gap widens across 2–3 audits ⇒ **you are reward-hacking**; rotate judges, harden rubric, shrink optimization pressure.

## Caveats

- **No published study tests Claude Opus/Sonnet self-preference specifically.** The quantitative self-preference numbers above come from GPT-4, Llama-4, DeepSeek-V3, and similar; generalization to the Opus-4.8/Sonnet-4.6 pairing is inferential (well-supported by the capability-scaling and perplexity-familiarity mechanisms, but not directly measured). Treat the same-family risk as established in direction and uncertain in exact magnitude.
- **Same-family bias as common-mode offset is a hypothesis, not a proven result for your setup.** It is the most defensible reading of the evidence (relative ranking among same-family competitors is less corrupted than absolute scores), but if your competitors diverge enough in style, the offset stops being common-mode and starts distorting ranks. The cross-family audit is how you check this empirically.
- **GEPA's headline numbers (35× fewer rollouts, +6–20%) are on specific benchmarks** (HotpotQA, IFBench, PUPA, HoVer, AIME-2025) with specific models (Qwen3-8B, GPT-4.1-mini). Your multi-turn agentic RAG task may not see the same gains; benchmark on your own data.
- **RAGAS reliability is genuinely contested.** The aggregate-correlation-but-poor-edge-case-calibration finding means RAGAS scores can look fine in aggregate while being wrong on the specific cases that matter. Do not over-trust a single RAGAS threshold.
- **Multi-Agent Debate does not reliably beat strong single agents** despite intuition; don't add debate machinery expecting free gains.
- **The "no human in the loop" goal is in tension with the "detect reward hacking" requirement.** A fully autonomous single-judge system *cannot* self-certify that it isn't gaming its own judge without some external anchor (gold slice or cross-family judge). Budget for that anchor or accept undetectable drift.
